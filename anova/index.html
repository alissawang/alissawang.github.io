Say we want to know whether there's any difference in milk production between brown, red, and black & white cows.

We sample 20 cows of each type and measure the number of gallons of milk they produced in a day:

Are the group samples different enough to reject the hypothesis that there is no difference between these types of cows?

We need to quantify how much of the differences we see are due to random error as opposed to actual differences between the means of each cow type.

The difference between a single sample point and the overall mean is partially explained by the difference between its group mean and the overall mean, which you might think of as the effect of "treatment":

And we might consider the difference between the sample point itself and the group mean as explained by sampling error:

The proportion of differences coming from the "treatment", or group type, vs error helps us determine whether there's a real difference due to the group type.

The example on the right has a much larger treatment-to-error proportion, which is more indicative of a real effect from group type.

We can apply this concept to the whole sample.

Sum of Squares Treatment (SST):

The squared difference between the overall mean and each group mean.

Sum of Squares Error (SSE):

The sum of squared differences between each sample point and its own group mean.

We then standardize our SST and SSE by dividing by their respective degrees of freedom.

:

The proportion of our standardized SST to SSE is the F-statistic.

We select an F distribution using our treatment and error degrees of freedom, and graph our F-statistic.

Assuming the default alpha value of 0.05, our result is not statistically significant. We therefore fail to reject the hypothesis that the average milk production is the same between all three cow types.
